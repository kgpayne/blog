> Give a person a fish and you feed them for a day.<br>
> Teach a person to fish and you feed them for a lifetime.<br>
<footer>
    \- [Anne Isabella Thackeray Ritchie](https://quoteinvestigator.com/2015/08/28/fish/)
</footer><br>

This piece of proverbial wisdom will hopefully be familiar to many, and, almost before we have begun to think about how it might apply to all things data, it offers insight; my Dad taught me to fish, and I have eaten almost every day since. The first lesson fishing has to teach us about data is correlation is different from causation. You could count the number of times I have eaten fish caught with my own fishing rod on one hand.

Whilst this nugget is certainly a useful reminder, it doesn’t set the insight bar particularly high. What I hope to illustrate in the following paragraphs is how Data Engineering is changing, and why _real_ fisherfolk will find these changes both familiar and rather obvious.

You have probably guessed by now that I am no expert fisherman. In my experience, fishing is as much about spending time on the water with Dad, chatting, drinking Coca Cola and waiting patiently for a turn in the ‘fighting chair' on the front of the boat (taking full command of the pedal-steered bass motor) as it is about catching supper. You might also deduce at this point that it’s been a while since my last fishing trip. Among many other things, I am allowed to drink beer now in stead of Coke. But, in defence of my 12 year old self, fishing is hard to learn and even harder to deliver on a schedule. This is where fishing and data engineering begin to look vaguely similar.

Data engineers have, until recently, been thought of as the proverbial fish-givers of the business world, catching fish on behalf of hungry people; fish in the form of datasets or "data products". It has been our mission to go out into the open lakes and oceans of 'the network' and catch data on behalf of the business. Inevitably this involves not only catching but also preparing and possibly even cooking said data. It is clear to me (sitting comfortably in the overlapping segments of the data engineer/novice fisherman ven diagram) that this almost exactly describes the typical 'extract, transform, load' activities that occupies the average data engineers time. The most common configuration of a Data Engineering team delivers <important>data-as-a-product</important>; one data source and integration at a time.

As implied in our proverb, 'giving people fish' does not scale gracefully. With more than a small handful of hungry would-be fish recipients our unenviable fisherfolk, faced with hauling in an ever-growing catch on a daily schedule, begin to reconsider their life choices. So what can be done? We are directed to "teach a person to fish" in order to solve our problems. Simple! Maybe not... Whilst this answer is neat in principle, it is much harder in practice. Fishing (as data engineering) is a specialism like any other, requiring time, experience and inclination to master. An organisation intent on training its entire staff to become part-time data engineers would almost certainly collapse under the weight of the endeavour.

So what really can be done? The second insight my (short) experience of fishing has to offer is this; some of the best fish Dad and I ever caught happened to arrive via a place like this...

Fish markets remain the single most viable strategy for the amateur fisherman to guarantee fame, glory and a full stomach. With a bit of sunburn as proof, almost any fish and accompanying tall tale is plausible. Success!

So, what does this mean for data engineering? By moving from fish suppliers to market hosts, data engineers can increase the quality, variety and availability of fish _without hiring an army of data engineers_. This approach is often referred to as <important>data-as-a-service</important>, and shifts our focus from writing ETL's and delivering data products to building a platform that directly connects producers with consumers. We build the market stalls, install fridges and ice chests and manage the obligatory 'catch of the day' chalkboard that directs hungry buyers to their perfect dinner. We also use our knowledge and experience of catching fish to help our market sellers to grow and manage their stocks. In stead of counting the fish we've caught, we measure success by:

- How easy it is to set up and own a market stall; catching, storing and selling fish (and fish-derived products).
- How easy it is find and buy the right fish for the dish.
- How easy it is to keep the market running smoothly.

The first-order benefits are clear, and many second-order benefits present themselves too. In a vibrant marketplace, expect to find not only an abundance of raw catch but also a growing selection of fish-derived delicacies and accompaniments; data that has been smoked, pickled, baked into pastries and otherwise prepared as even tastier treats.  In our online world, I hope to see an e-commerce styled data marketplace tool emerge to supply the growing need for freshly-caught data, alongside a smorgasbord of data-delicacies prepared by data analysts, scientists and other capable practitioners, all ready to be added to a virtual shopping cart and delivered to your virtual door for supper.

Whilst "teaching a person to fish" might not be the answer, "pointing a person to the fish market" certainly might 🐠
